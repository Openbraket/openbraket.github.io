Why Quantum Computing for Finance?

Certain financial workloads exhibit computational scaling challenges where quantum algorithms offer asymptotic advantages.

1. High-Dimensional State Representation

Quantum systems represent portfolio configurations in a Hilbert space of dimension 2n using n qubits, enabling parallel amplitude encoding. This does not replace classical memory, but enables new algorithmic primitives inaccessible to classical architectures.

2. Quadratic Monte Carlo Speedup (Asymptotic)

Classical Monte Carlo converges as O(1/√N), while Quantum Amplitude Estimation achieves O(1/N) query complexity. Practical realization on near-term hardware remains an active area of research.

3. Combinatorial Optimization Structure

Portfolio optimization with cardinality, transaction costs, and regulatory constraints leads to NP-hard problems. Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) provides a variational framework to explore these landscapes heuristically.

4. Emerging Hardware Capability

Modern superconducting and trapped-ion processors report >99.9% single-qubit gate fidelities in benchmark settings. Multi-qubit noise and circuit depth remain limiting factors, motivating hybrid quantum-classical workflows.

5. Regulatory and Computational Pressure

Stress testing and risk simulations required by Basel III / FRTB impose significant recurring computational costs on large institutions. Algorithmic acceleration is increasingly explored alongside hardware scaling.
